Altitude

"Over 2,000 meters?"

Higher farms are cooler. The cherry ripens slowly, so the seed packs in more sugars and acids and grows denser. That is why high-grown coffee tends to taste brighter and more complex. But it is a tendency, not a scoreboard: two of tonight's coffees clear the snob's bar, two do not, and all four are excellent.

Side by side

Blend vs single origin.

A blend combines several origins so every bag tastes the same: built for consistency, balance, and milk. A single origin is one farm, one harvest: it tastes like a place, and it changes with the seasons. Different jobs. Neither is better.

Taste: En Masse next to a single origin, back and forth

Sip the blend, sip the single origin, then the blend again. The difference shows up on the second pass.

Time3:30
Roast levels

Dark ≠ strong.

Roasting is a trade. The longer it goes: acidity fades, body builds, sugars caramelize, and the farm's flavor gives way to the roaster's flavor: chocolate, toast, smoke. On the darkest beans, oils rise to the surface, that is the shine.

Strength is set by how much coffee you brew per cup, not by the color. And caffeine between light and dark is basically a tie.

Taste or observe: En Masse (medium) vs Big Riff (dark)
SetupHold up both bags. The color difference is visible from across the room.
LandLonger roast = less acidity, more body, more roast flavor, oils surface. Strength = brew ratio. Caffeine ≈ tie.
PourShort on time? Just SHOW the two brews side by side and pass one sip of Big Riff. This is the slide to shorten.
Time3:00
How it gets brewed

Every method is three dials.

Grind, temperature, time. Hot water extracts fast. Cold water extracts slowly, so it borrows time instead. Pressure is a shortcut. And the grind matches the clock: fast methods want fine grounds, long methods want coarse.

Immersion

The grounds sit in the water. French press, cold brew. Forgiving, full-bodied.

Percolation

The water travels through the grounds. Pour over, drip. Cleaner, brighter.

Espresso is percolation in a hurry: nine bars of pressure, done in about 30 seconds. AeroPress is a steep with a push at the end. And a pod machine is a tiny drip brewer with the dials preset. It counts. They all count.

Setup"Everything from a pod machine to a 16-hour cold brew is the same three dials turned differently."
LandGrind + temperature + time. Immersion = sits in the water (press, cold brew). Percolation = passes through (pour over, drip). Espresso = pressure shortcut. Grind matches the clock.
PourNo new pour. If asked "what should I buy," the honest answer: whichever method fits your morning. This slide carries the thesis early: every dot on that line is a valid way to get coffee.

Water and freshness

About that water.

The Snob

"Captured in the ancient fjords of New Zealand."

The Casual

"It's green now."

Your cup is about 98% water, so water matters, and clean filtered tap is plenty. The freshness lever that actually matters is the roast date printed on the bag.

Fresh-roasted coffee is loaded with CO2 and fights the brew: gassy, uneven, sour-edged. After about a week it settles, holds a peak for a few weeks, then slowly fades. Tonight's bags: roasted June 22, poured July 6. That is 14 days rested, squarely in the filter sweet spot.

SetupBring back the fjord line word for word. Let the jar turn green in silence. Then flip.
Land98% water, filtered is plenty. Real lever = roast date. Too fresh = CO2 fights the brew. Peak ≈ week 1 to 3, then fades. 06/22 → 07/06 = 14 days, sweet spot.
PourHold up an actual bag and point at the printed date. Physical prop beats the slide.
